# Technical Summary: New IMAX 70mm Film Cameras ("Keighley") for Christopher Nolan's "The Odyssey"

## Overview

IMAX has developed a next-generation 70mm (15-perf 65mm) film camera system named **"Keighley"** — honoring David Keighley (IMAX's late Chief Quality Officer, who died Aug 28, 2025 at 77) and his wife Patricia Keighley (current Chief Quality Guru). The camera debuts on Christopher Nolan's *The Odyssey*, which is the **first theatrical feature ever shot 100% on IMAX film cameras**.

Four cameras were initially built. Nolan reserved all four for *The Odyssey*; they will be leased to other productions afterward.

---

## 1. The Development Story: How Nolan Challenged IMAX

### Nolan's Challenge to IMAX
After *Oppenheimer* — which earned $190M+ on IMAX screens (~20% of its total gross) — Nolan called IMAX CEO Rich Gelfond and said:

> *"If you can figure out how to solve the problems, I will make [Odyssey] 100 percent in IMAX."*

The "problems" were:
1. **Camera noise** — IMAX film cameras were too loud for dialogue scenes
2. **Weight and bulk** — makes them unwieldy for sustained shooting
3. **Dailies turnaround** — slow film scanning/processing made it hard to review footage quickly

### IMAX Investment
IMAX invested "millions of dollars" to build brand-new film cameras from the ground up. As Bruce Markoe (SVP, Head of Post Production and Image Capture at IMAX) stated at a 2024 SMPTE panel at NAB:

> *"They've taken longer than we would have liked unfortunately, but that's because they're a ground-up redesign of the camera... it's completely rebuilt and redesigned on the inside."*

## 2. The Keighley Camera: Technical Specs & Improvements

### Core Film Format (Unchanged)
- **Format:** 15-perforation 65mm film (horizontal pull-down through the gate)
- **Film movement:** The core film transport mechanism ("the movement") was intentionally **kept identical** to the previous generation — IMAX engineers decided not to re-engineer it to avoid breaking what already worked perfectly
- **Film stock:** Kodak 65mm color and black-and-white film
- **Magazine capacity:** 1,000-foot rolls (approx. 2.5–3 minutes of continuous shooting per magazine)
- **Total film shot for The Odyssey:** Over 2 million feet (~$3M at $1.50/foot from Kodak), over 91 shooting days

### Weight Reduction
- **Previous IMAX MSM9802 generation (used on Dunkirk/Oppenheimer):** ~25 kg / 55 lbs (body only, with 1,000-ft roll and accessories)
- **Keighley improvement:** Carbon fiber honeycomb sandwich panels and titanium construction (same materials used in Formula 1 and jet fighters)
- **Result:** Significantly lighter body that improves ergonomics for operators
- **However, when fitted with the soundproof "blimp" housing:** Total system weight reaches approximately **180 kg / ~400 lbs** (300+ lbs per Matt Damon, 180 kg per French source)
- The blimped camera required **specially fabricated steel plating on dollies** to support the weight

### Noise Reduction (The "Blimp" System)

This is the **single most important technical breakthrough**:

- **30% reduction in camera mechanism noise** through internal design improvements (better bearings, dampening)
- **External "blimp" housing** (also called a "coffin" by crew) — a massive soundproof acoustic enclosure wrapped around the camera
- The blimp is described as **"the size of a coffin"** weighing **300+ lb / ~180 kg** fully assembled
- Combined effect: The system is quiet enough that Nolan reports: **"You can be shooting a foot from [an actor's] face while they're whispering and get usable sound."**
- This eliminates the need for ADR (automatic dialogue replacement) for IMAX dialogue scenes — a major limitation on prior Nolan films

### Mirror System for Eyelines
Because the blimped camera is so large, the actors' eyelines (line of sight) became too extreme for dialogue scenes. IMAX engineers built a **mirror system** that allowed actors to stand next to the camera, look at a mirror reflecting back to the lens, while maintaining a natural eyeline close to the optical path.

### New Optical Viewfinder (Panavision)
- Designed and built in collaboration with **Panavision**
- Two configurations: short eyepiece (handheld) and extended eyepiece (studio/dolly)
- Built-in **4K UHD tap** sensor — sends live video via SDI to monitors and the cloud
- Metadata-capable
- Aspect ratio overlays (1.43:1, 1.90:1, 2.40:1) for composing different exhibition formats

### Connectivity & Monitoring
- **Gigabit Ethernet, WiFi, Bluetooth** onboard
- **4K tap output** can be sent wirelessly to iPads, monitors, or directly to the cloud for remote viewing
- **Dailies recording:** The 4K feed can be recorded and reviewed without waiting for film processing — a massive workflow improvement
- Three SDI video ports, two 2-pin power ports for accessories

### Software/UI
- **5-inch full-color LCD display** with updated physical buttons
- Film management readout showing:
  - Remaining footage in magazine (e.g., 980/1,000 ft)
  - Exposed footage (e.g., 20 ft)
  - Total project footage
  - **Estimated remaining run time** (critical for the ~2.5-min reload window)
  - Frame rate display
  - Battery voltage
- Customizable user profiles
- Remote control capability

### Materials
- **Carbon fiber honeycomb sandwich panels** (same as Formula 1 chassis)
- **Titanium components** (same as jet fighter construction)
- Composite materials throughout for weight reduction

## 3. Comparison with Previous IMAX Cameras (Dunkirk/Oppenheimer Era)

| Feature | Legacy IMAX (MSM9802, ~1990s design) | New Keighley (2025) |
|---------|--------------------------------------|---------------------|
| **Design era** | 1970s roots; MSM9802 ~25 years old | Ground-up redesign (first new IMAX film camera since late 1990s) |
| **Body materials** | Metal | Carbon fiber composite + titanium |
| **Weight (body only)** | ~25 kg / 55 lb | Reduced, exact figure not specified |
| **Blimped weight** | N/A (no blimp existed) | ~180 kg / 400 lb |
| **Noise level** | Very loud (described as "blender"/"Cuisinart") | ~30% quieter internal mechanism + blimp housing |
| **Video tap** | Low-res, poor quality for focus/exposure | 4K UHD via SDI, cloud-capable |
| **Viewfinder** | Optical only | Optical + Panavision-designed with 4K tap |
| **Connectivity** | None | Gigabit Ethernet, WiFi, Bluetooth |
| **Menu/Display** | Mechanical readout | 5-inch color LCD, customizable |
| **Metadata** | None | Full metadata capture |
| **Remote control** | No | Yes, with user profiles |
| **Dailies** | Film must be processed/scanned first | 4K tap recorded for instant review |
| **Sync dialogue** | Impossible without ADR | Possible (blimp system) |
| **Quantity built** | ~5-10 units worldwide | 4 units initially |

### Previous Nolan IMAX Usage
- **The Dark Knight (2008):** ~30 min shot on IMAX film (action sequences)
- **Interstellar (2014):** ~60 min on IMAX film
- **Dunkirk (2017):** ~70 min on IMAX film (~75% of runtime)
- **Tenet (2020):** ~80 min on IMAX film
- **Oppenheimer (2023):** Extensive IMAX, including first-ever IMAX 65mm B&W film stock (developed with Kodak), but still **not 100%** — dialogue scenes shot with other formats
- **The Odyssey (2026):** **100% IMAX 15-perf 65mm film** — first feature film ever

## 4. How It Was Tested & Proven

### The David Bowie "Sound and Vision" Test
Cinematographer **Hoyte van Hoytema** shot test footage of a child reciting the lyrics to David Bowie's "Sound and Vision" using the new blimped IMAX camera. He projected it as a massive close-up on an IMAX screen. The combination of intimate image quality and clean recorded dialogue convinced Nolan. His reaction:

> *"It was electrifying. We never would have been able to get those shots before."*

### Production Details
- **Filmed over 91 days** (principal photography wrapped August 2025)
- **2 million+ feet of 65mm film** used (~$3M in raw stock)
- Majority of filming took place **at sea**, on real locations
- The 2.5-minute reload limit remained a challenge — extreme discipline was needed per take

## 6. Impact on the Industry

- **Denis Villeneuve** has already used the new IMAX 70mm cameras for scenes in **Dune: Part Three**
- Previously, filmmakers had to choose between IMAX film (spectacular but loud/unwieldy) and digital (quiet but lower-acuity). The Keighley blimp system **eliminates that trade-off for dialogue scenes**
- The camera is now on **public display at IMAX HQ**
- 70mm film screenings for *The Odyssey* sold out within an hour (95% of seats), generating $1.5M across just 26 theaters — a year in advance

---

## 7. Remaining Limitations

- **2.5-minute reloads** are unchanged — the format's massive 15-perf frame size limits magazine capacity
- The **blimped system is extremely heavy** (~180 kg), requiring special dollies and limiting mobility
- **Only 4 units exist**, and they are reserved for Nolan's production initially
- The camera still requires **analog photochemical post-production** at FotoKem (Burbank, CA) — the only lab in the world still producing 70mm prints — with hand-splicing of negatives and analog color timing

# Ludwig Göransson's Score for "The Odyssey" (2026) — Research Findings

## Overview

- **Film:** *The Odyssey*, directed by Christopher Nolan, releasing July 17, 2026
- **Composer:** Ludwig Göransson (third collaboration with Nolan after *Tenet* and *Oppenheimer*)
- **Budget:** ~$250 million (Nolan's most expensive film)
- **Shot:** Entirely on IMAX 15/70mm film cameras (first narrative feature to do so)
- **91 shooting days** across six countries: Morocco, Greece, Italy, Iceland, Scotland, and Universal lot (Falls Lake water tank, USA)

---

## 1. No Orchestra — Nolan's Key Instruction

Nolan explicitly instructed Göransson **not to use a traditional orchestra** for the score. This was a deliberate creative choice to subvert expectations for a swords-and-sandals epic.

**Key quote from Göransson (to Time magazine):**
> "It's not like the orchestra existed back then. It was a challenge and also an opening to try to make something unique."

**Key context from Göransson (to Esquire, March 2026):**
> He teases that he's debuting "a whole new thing" in the upcoming blockbuster, but whatever ancient Greek traditions or unheard-of island seashell ocarinas he immersed himself in for this film are momentarily his secrets to keep. "When Troy's gates open, Göransson promises you'll hear it."

**Sources:** Time magazine (May 12, 2026), Esquire (March 5, 2026), MusicRadar (May 15, 2026)

---

## 2. Bronze Gongs + Synthesizers

Instead of an orchestra, Göransson **rented 35 bronze gongs of varying sizes**. He experimented with them and then recorded them layered with synthesizers.

- The gongs are described as "period appropriate" (bronze being a Bronze Age material), while the synthesizers are acknowledged as anachronistic.
- The combination creates a sound that "thunders, shivers, and lingers like salt on skin" (3DVF description).
- Göransson **sent Nolan songs** built from these experiments, developing the score iteratively.
- The gong + synth approach reflects the film's broader production philosophy: mixing authentic ancient elements with modern storytelling techniques.

**Sources:** Time magazine, MusicRadar, Yahoo Entertainment, 3DVF, Variety

---

## 3. The Lyre Sound for Odysseus's Bow

A **lyre** (an ancient Greek string instrument, nearly the size of a grown man, seen in Göransson's studio) was used for a specific narrative purpose.

**Key quote from Göransson (to Time):**
> "Chris had this idea of the sound of the lyre being the pluck of Odysseus' bow."

This connects the instrument directly to Odysseus's weapon — the bow he uses to kill the suitors. The lyre's plucked strings sonically evoke the bowstring's tension and release.

The lyre sits prominently in Göransson's studio, described by Time as being "one room over from a ping-pong table that, at the push of a button, disappears into the floor," illustrating the blend of ancient and modern.

**Sources:** Time magazine, Yahoo Entertainment, CBR

---

## 4. Travis Scott as Bard — Oral Poetry & Rap Connection

Rapper **Travis Scott** was cast as a bard in the film, which directly ties into the musical and storytelling philosophy.

**Key quote from Nolan (to Time):**
> "I cast him because I wanted to nod towards the idea that this story has been handed down as oral poetry, which is analogous to rap."

- Scott previously contributed "The Plan" to Nolan's *Tenet* (2020).
- Nolan told *GQ* in 2020 that Scott "became the final piece of a yearlong puzzle" for *Tenet* and credited Göransson for wanting to involve Scott.
- Scott confirmed his role via a TV spot during the January 2026 AFC Championship Game, alongside Tom Holland and Jon Bernthal.
- The bard role connects Homeric tradition (oral poetry performed/sung) with contemporary hip-hop performance.

**Sources:** Billboard (May 12, 2026), Time, Variety, CBR

## 5. Trailer/Marketing — Early Descriptions of the Score

### First Trailer (December 22, 2025)
Described by **Trailaurality** as featuring:
- **0-20 seconds:** "Eternal, guttural, wordless vocal utterance" over a battlefield scene, a "minor sixth to perfect fifth motif"
- **Mid-section:** A soundscape of "synths, brass, vocals, perhaps strings — all mixed in a vast, meditative drone"
- **1:05:** "Incisive harmonic inflection" that helps maintain energy
- Chords moving between "C major and the parallel, frigid Phrygian (minor, with that added Db minor second)" with epic percussion
- **1:32:** The minor sixth motif returns as Odysseus's ship is caught in a storm, with no resolution
- The trailer ends with the C-Ab minor sixth in brass along with a guttural sound "perhaps evoking one of the many creatures"

**Esquire (March 2026) described the first trailer thus:**
> "Göransson's score was merely hinted at with war horns, a full chorus, and pounding synthesizers."

### The IMAX Prologue (December 2025)
A 6-minute prologue played before IMAX screenings of *Avatar: Fire and Ash* and *Sinners*.

**World of Reel description:**
> "Ludwig Göransson's score ramping up, getting more and more intense, with brooding chants amplifying as the battle goes on. It's unlike any composition he's done before."

**The Daily Telegraph's Tim Robey** described Göransson's music for the prologue's Trojan Horse sequence as "building a nerve-racking crescendo throughout."

**MovieWeb description:**
> "The score is propulsive and thrilling to match the intensity and suspense of the siege, giving audiences the same excitement as they did when they heard the first segments of Hans Zimmer's *The Dark Knight* score."

### Second Trailer (May 5, 2026)
**The Marquee description:**
> "Driven by a metronomic, ticking score that feels like the frantic evolution of Göransson's Oscar-winning textures [from *Oppenheimer*]."

### CinemaCon 2026 (April 15)
Nolan presented footage at CinemaCon. No specific score details were reported beyond the general descriptions above.

### Göransson's Own Comments (January 2026)
On *Good Hang with Amy Poehler*, Göransson confirmed from his Los Angeles studio that he was "in the world of Chris Nolan right now," working on *The Odyssey* score, describing it as "taking up [his] time" but "fun."

## 6. Recording Techniques & Studio Approach

While specific recording session details are limited, several facts are known:

- **Göransson's studio** houses a full-size lyre alongside modern equipment and a ping-pong table — symbolizing the ancient/modern blend.
- The score was developed by **experimenting with the 35 bronze gongs**, recording them, and then layering with **synthesizers**.
- Göransson **sent Nolan songs** built from these experiments as the score developed.
- The composer's signature approach — deeply tied to the film's concept — involves using unconventional instruments and techniques, as seen in his previous Nolan scores (*Tenet* used reversed audio, *Oppenheimer* used time signatures to emulate quantum mechanics).
- Unlike Göransson's concurrent work on *The Mandalorian and Grogu* (which used a 104-piece orchestra), *The Odyssey* deliberately avoids orchestral arrangements entirely.

---

## 7. The Sirens — Psycho-Analytical Song

A notable musical element revealed by the Time interview: the **Sirens** in the film don't just sing — they **psycho-analyze Odysseus through song**. This suggests the score incorporates diegetic musical performances as well as the underscore, with the Sirens' music serving a narrative/psychological function.

## Summary

The score for *The Odyssey* represents a radical departure from traditional epic film scoring. Nolan explicitly forbade an orchestra, so Göransson used **35 bronze gongs** (recorded and layered with synths), a **lyre** (whose sound represents the pluck of Odysseus's bow), wordless vocals/chorus, and war horns. Rapper Travis Scott appears as a bard, connecting oral poetry tradition to hip-hop. Early descriptions of the score from the trailers and IMAX prologue describe it as propulsive, brooding, with guttural chants, meditative drones, and a "nerve-racking crescendo." The full score has not been released yet (the film opens July 17, 2026), but it is already generating significant Oscar buzz.
